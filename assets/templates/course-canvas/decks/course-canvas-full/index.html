DEFINITION

Etymology From Latin norma — a carpenter's square, the standard against which things are measured.
KEY CONCEPT

Normal distribution

正态分布
DEFINITION · n. A symmetric, bell-shaped spread of values where data clusters around the mean and thins out evenly toward both tails — fully described by just two numbers: its center (μ) and its spread (σ).
In plain words
Most results land near average; the further you go from the middle, the rarer they get.
You'll see it in
Heights, test scores, measurement error, daily temperatures.

STEP-BY-STEP

Turning a value into a z-score

  1. Find the centerWrite down the mean μ of the dataset.
  2. Measure the gapSubtract the mean from your value: x − μ.
  3. Scale by spreadDivide by the standard deviation σ.
  4. Read the resultThe z-score is how many σ from the mean you are.
Worked once
z = x − μ σ
x = 86   μ = 74   σ = 8
z = (86 − 74) / 8 = 1.5
Teaching cue Pause after step 3 — have students predict the sign of z before you reveal the number.

COMMON MISTAKES

Where this goes wrong

✕ MISCONCEPTION
“A bigger z-score always means a bigger raw value.”

Forgets that z can be negative — and depends on σ, not just x.

✓ CORRECT
A z-score measures distance from the mean in σ units — direction and scale both matter.

z = −2 is just as far out as z = +2.

✕
Forgetting to divide by σ. Subtracting the mean isn't enough — you must scale.
✕
Assuming every dataset is normal. Skewed data breaks the 68–95–99.7 rule.

What to take away

HANDOUT · PRINTABLE
1
The normal curve is symmetric and set by two numbers — the mean μ and spread σ.
2
The 68–95–99.7 rule tells you how much data sits within 1, 2 and 3 σ.
3
A z-score rewrites any value as “how many σ from the mean”.
KEY TERMS · GLOSSARY
mean μ
the balancing center of the data
std. dev σ
the typical distance from the mean
z-score
(x − μ) ÷ σ — distance in σ units
empirical rule
68 / 95 / 99.7 within 1 / 2 / 3 σ
